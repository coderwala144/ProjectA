Sugercane

 Sugarcane, a giant tropical grass, is grown in over a hundred countries by independent farmers and agroindustrial firms. It is increasingly being sought after by pharmaceutical, chemical, and energy firms. CIRAD and its partners are helping sugarcane value chains evolve and diversify to meet growing demand. Sugarcane, originating in Papua New Guinea, belongs to the Graminaceae family and the botanical genus Saccharum. Its main use is in industrial processing of stalks to make rum, but its plant mass can also be converted into energy, charcoal, or biofuel. Sugarcane plants have long, narrow leaves that produce plant matter, including sugar, and are rich in cellulose and lignin, which have various uses in the green chemicals industry. Sugarcane is a "C4" plant, absorbing more carbon dioxide and sunlight than other plants, resulting in luxuriant vegetation and high sugar content. The history of sugar began in India 5000 years ago, with traders selling sugar in the shape of crystallized loaves.

UGES:

  • SUGER:Sugarcane is primarily used to produce refined sugar, brown sugar, molasses, and jaggery.
  • ENERGY: Sugarcane can be used to make charcoal, combustible material, and biofuel.
  • CHEMICALS:Sugarcane contains molecules that are used in the chemicals industry.
  • PAPER:The pulp from sugarcane can be used to make paper.
  • TEXTTILES:The dried fiber left over after extracting sugarcane juice, called bagasse, can be used in textile production.
  • LIVESTOCK: Sugarcane leaves can be used as fodder for livestock.
  • FERTILIZER:The filter mud produced during the filtration of sugarcane juice can be used as fertilizer because of its nitrogen content.
